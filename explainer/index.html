1 · The state is typed, not just big

The scene above holds one urban state 𝒵 = {ℒ, ℋ, 𝒜, 𝒟, ℱ, 𝒮, 𝒴}: land use (the orange job shading and household dots), demand 𝒟 (the desire lines), path flows ℱ (the column pool the solver builds), and network state 𝒮 (the road widths and colors). Each axis has a role — land use is a boundary condition, paths are generated by the algorithm, flows are the unknown, and observations would enter as operators, never by overwriting state. That typing is what lets the same objects scale from this toy to a 33,963-node regional model with interfaces unchanged.

→ try: balanced town — with jobs everywhere, desire lines are short and the network barely notices.

2 · Land use → demand is a kernel, not a file

Hover any zone: its desire lines are the gravity kernel Tij ∝ Pi ej e−βcij evaluated live — which is identically a logit destination choice with utility Vj = ln ej − βcij, and, in operator form, 𝒟 = ∫ KLA ℒ. Slide β and watch trips shorten: the kernel, not a trip table, is the object.

→ try: monocentric rush hour, then click an outer zone to plant a subcenter and watch demand re-route before any traffic is assigned.

3 · Equilibrium is a fixed point you can watch

Press slow motion. Each iteration does exactly four things: (1) price every road at current flows (BPR: t = t₀(1 + 0.15(v/c)⁴)); (2) find shortest paths for every OD pair (all-origin Dijkstra — over the full network, never a stored path pool); (3) send everyone to those paths and blend with step 1/k (MSA), which also grows the column pool; (4) compute the relative gap — how much total travel time could still fall if everyone switched. When the gap crosses the tolerance the badge lights: that is a certificate of equilibrium, the same quantity at every scale of the released suite (Chicago 9.8e-5; regional 4.4e-7). It attests convergence — not calibration.

→ try: run, then drag the iteration scrubber backwards to watch congestion smooth itself out.

4 · Click a road: the math is in the picture

Every road carries its own live equation. Click one after running: the panel shows BPR filled with that road's current numbers, its volume/capacity bar, and the origin–destination pairs actually using it (read off the column pool — one entity, every view). Orange roads are nearing capacity; magenta ones are over it, and in the full framework that is where queues form and the time axis takes over (fluid-queue card on the home page).

5 · The same objects at real scale

Nothing in this scene is a cartoon of the real solver — it is the real solver, small: column generation with full-space pricing and an MSA blend. The released package runs the identical contract on Chicago Sketch (93,135 ODs, gap 9.8e-5, link flows correlating 1.0000 with reference volumes) and a full region (1,039,117 ODs, 4.4e-7, single thread). The browser engine here certifies a 5×5 city in ~20 iterations and an 8×8 with 1,500+ OD pairs in under 100 ms.
